## The bill had real internal turbulence, not a clean party-line fight
Vote totals swung wildly across stages:
- House original passage (3/10): 53–42
- House "Concur in Senate Amendments" (weaker Senate text): **failed 48–50**
- House "Grant Conference": 58–40
- House final passage (conference/compromise version): 54–44
- Senate original passage (amended): 29–20
- Senate final passage (conference version): 27–20

That reversal pattern (the weaker Senate version got rejected, then a stronger conference compromise passed) means a chunk of members flipped their vote between stages depending on bill strength — those flips are your best signal of persuadability.

## Republicans: essentially a locked "no" bloc on this bill
Across every recorded motion, **zero House or Senate Republicans voted Yea on final passage**, and none crossed over even on the weaker Senate-amendment version. On this specific rent-cap approach, GOP votes look locked-in, not persuadable — outreach dollars are probably better spent elsewhere for this bill (though narrower, consensus tenant bills like **HB 2452**, service-of-rent-notice rules, passed nearly unanimously 96‑0/48‑0, showing Republicans will engage on incremental tenant-protection fixes).

**Senate — actively tried to reshape the bill via floor amendments, not just vote no:**

**Senate — backed weakening amendments but still voted yes in the end (useful for locking in support and shaping implementation/future bills):**
